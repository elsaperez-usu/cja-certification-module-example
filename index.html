Litigation: Post-Judgment
Collecting the debt — and what your client can still do

A judgment entered. The creditor now has legal tools to take your client's money. This guide walks you through what happens next — supplemental proceedings, garnishment, exemptions, the motions that can change the picture, and how a judgment finally ends.

What This Module Trains You To Do

A judgment is not a bill — it is a court order with the force of law. But entry of judgment is not the end of the story. Exemptions exist, motions exist, and deadlines run in both directions. Your job is to know which protections apply, which windows are still open, and when to call your supervising attorney.

Learning objectives — check each one off as you master it:

  • ✓Explain what a judgment means and the two kinds of creditor tools: identification (supplemental proceedings) and seizure (garnishment, execution, liens).
  • ✓Prepare a client for a supplemental proceeding, including the 3-day written-answers option and the consequences of not appearing.
  • ✓Work the 5-step Garnishment Response Checklist — Identify, Gather, Assess, Advise, Escalate — for both wage and bank account garnishment.
  • ✓Calculate disposable earnings correctly and catch employer errors (25% cap, $217.50 weekly floor, mandatory deductions only).
  • ✓Identify exempt income and property and explain why exemptions are not self-executing — someone must claim them within 14 days.
  • ✓Trace exempt deposits in a frozen bank account, including joint account holders' independent rights.
  • ✓Analyze a Motion to Set Aside through the three gates — grounds, timing, meritorious defense — and pair it with a Motion to Stay when enforcement is running.
  • ✓Determine the correct post-judgment interest rate and explain renewal (8 years) and satisfaction of judgment.

The Substance

Key Concepts, Section by Section

What a judgment means

An official court decision that your client owes money — a court order with the force of law. Creditors then use two kinds of tools: Identification supplemental proceedings, and Seizure garnishment and property seizure. Collection cannot start for 28 days after entry. A Utah judgment lasts 8 years and can be renewed for 8 more.

Silver lining worth remembering: post-judgment interest (currently 5.51% statutory) is often far lower than pre-judgment payday-loan rates. Entry of judgment is not always a catastrophe.

Section 1 — Supplemental proceedings

The creditor won, but doesn't yet know what it can take. A supplemental proceeding compels your client to answer questions about income and property. Key points:

The 3-day option

If your client answers the written questions and delivers them at least 3 days before the scheduled hearing, the creditor may cancel it — less pressure, more prep time, no courthouse trip.

If your client doesn't show

A bench warrant can issue. The constable typically collects money toward the debt — and your client still has to answer the questions.

The answers form is not filed with the court — court records are public, and filing it would expose all of your client's private financial information.

Section 2 — Garnishment: two writs, two processes

Wage garnishment

Writ of Continuing Garnishment → goes to the employer (the garnishee). Repeats every pay period. Capped at 25% of disposable earnings; $217.50/week floor. Lasts 1 year (or 120 days if a second writ is served).

Bank account garnishment

Writ of Garnishment → goes to the bank. One-time seizure of whatever is in the account the day the writ is served. No 25% cap — the entire balance is at risk, minus what your client proves is exempt.

Other tools: a Writ of Execution (constable seizes and sells property in your client's hands — rare, often used as pressure; escalate immediately) and a judgment lien (Abstract of Judgment recorded with the County Recorder; blocks sale or refinance of real property; always refer lien questions to your supervising attorney).

Exemptions — the shield someone has to pick up

Protected income Social Security, SSI, VA disability & death benefits, workers' comp, public assistance, unemployment, child support received, qualified retirement distributions.

Protected property A personal vehicle up to a set value, tools of the trade, basic household furnishings, and the home up to the homestead exemption.

Exemptions are not self-executing. The creditor doesn't check. The constable doesn't check. If no one claims the exemption within 14 days, the money goes to the creditor. That someone is often you.

Section 3 — Motion to Delay (Stay) Enforcement

Pauses all collection while a Motion to Set Aside is decided. Two things to understand immediately: it is not a remedy on its own — it only buys time, so it must travel with a Motion to Set Aside — and the court will almost certainly require a bond. Whether your client can post one is the main practical question. Low income and defenses backed by more than mere allegations both weigh toward a smaller bond.

Section 4 — Motion to Set Aside (URCP 60(b))

Asks the court to undo the judgment. If granted, the case reopens and your client gets to defend. Before analyzing, always find out why the judgment entered — failure to answer, failure to respond to RFAs, or failure to appear — because the motion usually must be accompanied by a proposed version of the missed filing (a proposed answer, or proposed RFA responses). Then work the three gates, in order. See the Three Gates section below.

Section 5 — How judgments continue or end

Post-judgment interest runs from the day of entry. Three questions find the rate: (1) formal payday loan under the Deferred Deposit Act? → federal rate + 2%. (2) Contract case where the judgment specifies a rate? → that contract rate controls. (3) Under $10,000 for the purchase of goods or services (not a loan)? → federal rate + 10%; otherwise federal rate + 2%. Use the federal rate from the year the judgment entered.

Renewal: before the 8 years expire, a creditor can file a Motion to Renew (district court only — justice/small claims judgments require a new lawsuit under § 78B-6-1802). The renewal moment is a natural settlement opening.

Satisfaction: when the debt is paid — in full or in a negotiated amount accepted as complete — the creditor must file a Satisfaction of Judgment, plus a County Recorder filing in every county where an Abstract was recorded. If they don't, your client can file a motion to compel/declare satisfaction.

The Clocks That Decide Cases

Every Deadline in Module 5

Post-judgment practice is a set of running clocks. Maria lost money because of a 14-day window she didn't understand. Memorize these — red markers are hard deadlines where missing means losing something irreversibly.

3 days before the supplemental hearing
Deliver written answers to the creditor's questions by this point and the hearing may be canceled.
28 days after judgment entry
Collection cannot begin before this waiting period ends. After it, garnishment and seizure tools open up.
14 days from receiving the Notice of Garnishment
Your client's window to file a Reply and Request for Hearing to claim exemptions or dispute the amount. The clock runs from when the Notice was mailed or delivered — not when your client read it or called you. Joint account holders have their own independent 14-day clock.
Miss it → money already sent to the creditor is gone. A late Reply only affects the future.
21 days before the garnishee can send money
The employer or bank must hold funds for 21 days after serving the Notice. If a Reply is filed within the 14-day window, the money stays held while the court decides. Notice how the two clocks nest: your client's deadline comes first.
7 business days after each pay period
The employer must complete the Garnishee's Answers to Interrogatories — the form you compare against your own math in Step 3.
3 months from judgment entry
Hard deadline for a Motion to Set Aside on grounds 1, 2, and 3 (mistake/excusable neglect, newly discovered evidence, fraud). Runs from entry, not discovery. Grounds 4, 5, and 6 have no three-month cap — but every ground still requires filing within a "reasonable time."
Miss it → grounds 1–3 are time-barred no matter how strong they are.
1 year (or 120 days)
A Writ of Continuing Garnishment expires 1 year from service — shortened to 120 days if a second continuing writ is served. The writ ends; the judgment does not. The creditor can get a new writ.
8 years
A Utah judgment is enforceable for 8 years. Before expiration the creditor can file a Motion to Renew (district court) for another 8 — or must file a new lawsuit for justice court / small claims judgments.
For creditors, that is. For your client, renewal is a natural settlement opening.
Your Clinical Tool

The 5-Step Garnishment Response Checklist

Identify → Gather → Assess → Advise → Escalate. The module trains the substance; the checklist is what you use in the room with your client. Here is each step for both writ types.

1 · Identify — which writ is this?

"My paycheck was smaller" → wage garnishment (Writ of Continuing Garnishment). "My account is frozen / my card was declined" → bank account garnishment (Writ of Garnishment). Then: when did your client receive the Notice of Garnishment and Exemptions? Count the 14 days. If the writ names a former employer, flag it — it may not be valid.

2 · Gather — the document set

Wage: Notice of Garnishment and Exemptions, the Writ, the Reply form, Garnishee's Answers to Interrogatories, pay stubs. Bank: the same notice set plus at least two months of bank statements — you cannot trace exempt funds without them. Note anything missing; a missing Notice is an escalation trigger.

3 · Assess — do the math / trace the funds

Wage: disposable earnings = gross pay minus mandatory deductions only (federal tax, state tax, FICA, Medicare — that is the complete list; never health insurance, 401(k), or union dues). Withholding = the lesser of 25% of disposable earnings or the amount above the $217.50/week floor. Bank: match each deposit to its source; identify exempt deposits; choose a tracing method (first-in-first-out vs. last-in-first-out) that honestly reflects which funds remain.

4 · Advise — the full picture

What ends a continuing writ: time, payment in full, or court order. The writ names a specific employer and can't follow a job change — but supplemental proceedings can find the new job. Only one continuing writ runs at a time (child support writs jump the line). Discuss budget impact, settlement (lump sums still talk, even post-judgment), and bankruptcy referral (outside CJA scope — ask your attorney).

5 · Escalate — when to call your supervising attorney

The math looks wrong and you're not confident in your calculation · a required form never arrived · mixed exempt/non-exempt deposits make tracing unclear · a Writ of Execution or lien appears · child support garnishment enters the picture · anything outside your training. Have ready: the Notice, your calculation beside the employer's, exemption notes, and pay stubs or statements.

Motion to Set Aside

The Three-Gate Analysis

Work the gates in order. Each must be satisfied before the next matters. If one gate looks weak, do not stop — complete the analysis and bring all of it to your supervising attorney.

1

Grounds

Is there a URCP 60(b) basis? Most common in debt cases: Ground 1 — mistake, inadvertence, surprise, or excusable neglect; Ground 3 — fraud or misconduct by the plaintiff (bad service address, false filings); Ground 4 — the judgment is void (never served → no jurisdiction). Also: Ground 2 (newly discovered evidence), Ground 5 (already satisfied), Ground 6 (catch-all, used sparingly). More than one may apply — write them all down.

Gate closes → no legal basis, motion fails.
2

Timing

Two requirements stack. Grounds 1–3: hard 3-month deadline from entry of judgment — not from discovery. Grounds 4–6: no 3-month cap. Every ground: filed within a reasonable time — the gap between when your client learned of the basis and when they acted must be explainable.

Gate closes → time-barred, no matter how strong the grounds.
3

Meritorious Defense

If reopened, is there anything worth trying? Counts: statute of limitations had run, debt already paid (in full or part), wrong amount, wrong person, debt buyer can't prove chain of title. Does not count: hardship, unfairness, regret — real, but not legal defenses.

Gate closes → court may deny even a timely, well-grounded motion.
Don't forget the companions: a proposed version of whatever filing was missed (proposed answer, or proposed RFA responses — a client who already answered, like Maria, may need nothing new); a Motion to Stay if enforcement is running, with a bond analysis; and the settlement checkpoint before filing — ask your client whether they can offer a lump sum, ask the plaintiff what settlement could look like, and ask whether the plaintiff will stipulate to the motions. The CJA facilitates communication but never agrees to anything on the client's behalf.

Case Studies & Exercises

PracticeExercise 1 · Maria's pay stub — is the employer's math right?▶
Maria earns $14.00/hour and works 20 hours/week at Fresh Market Utah. Her employer has been withholding $70.00 per week. Her pay stub shows: federal income tax $14.00 · Utah state income tax $13.02 · Social Security $17.36 · Medicare $4.06 · health insurance $25.00.
  1. What is Maria's gross weekly pay?
  2. Which deductions count toward disposable earnings? List each and its amount.
  3. Calculate Maria's disposable earnings.
  4. Is the $70.00 withholding correct? Calculate the correct amount, showing every step.
  5. If her disposable earnings were $210.00/week instead, what would change — and why?

Self-check

(1) $280.00 (20 hrs × $14.00). (2) Federal income tax $14.00, Utah state income tax $13.02, Social Security $17.36, Medicare $4.06. The $25.00 health insurance is voluntary and must not be included. (3) $280.00 − $48.44 = $231.56.

(4) Correct withholding is the lesser of 25% × $231.56 = $57.89, or the amount above the floor: $231.56 − $217.50 = $14.06. The employer withheld $70.00 — five times the lawful amount. They appear to have treated gross pay as disposable earnings and ignored the floor. This is a meaningful error that harms Maria, and exactly what Step 3 exists to catch.

(5) $210.00 is below the $217.50 floor — nothing could be garnished at all. The floor protects the entire paycheck regardless of what the creditor is owed.

ReflectExercise 2 · Maria calls on Day 16▶
Maria received the Notice of Garnishment and Exemptions on a Tuesday. She put it aside because she didn't understand it. She calls you on Day 16.
  1. What happened to the money withheld during those 16 days?
  2. The Reply form is still available. Can it recover money already sent to the creditor?
  3. What can the Reply still do for Maria going forward?

Self-check

After 14 days, the employer may send withheld money to the creditor. Once sent, it is gone — a Reply cannot recover it. The Reply affects only future pay periods. Maria can still file after the deadline, but she loses protection for amounts already remitted. This is a one-time window; missing it is not reversible.

ReflectExercise 3 · "Does this mean they'll look at the case again?"▶
After filing her Reply, Maria calls you: "I filed the form. Does this mean they have to look at the case again? Can they undo the judgment now?"
  1. What did filing the Reply actually do? Be specific.
  2. What would have to happen for the judgment itself to be challenged?
  3. What should Maria expect on her next paycheck — and the one after that?

Self-check

The Reply addresses the garnishment only — claiming exemptions or disputing the amount. It does not reopen the case, challenge the judgment, or recover money already sent. Challenging the judgment requires a Motion to Set Aside under URCP 60(b) — an entirely separate motion. Unless the court orders otherwise, the garnishment continues; if Maria has exempt income, the Reply may reduce future withholding. The judgment stands until something changes that.

ReflectExercise 4 · Carl & Sarah's frozen account▶
The writ was served March 15; the joint account shows $2,437.22 frozen that day. On March 10, two paychecks hit the same day: Carl's $1,240 (Vantage Solutions) and Sarah's $1,180. Sarah is a joint account holder but not the judgment debtor. Carl's next paycheck arrives March 24.
  1. What right does Sarah have to protect her money in the frozen account?
  2. Carl is sitting across from you. What is the single most important thing to tell him to do in the next 24 hours — and why?
  3. How much of the frozen balance is traceable to Sarah's paycheck, and why does that matter?
  4. Is the March 24 deposit subject to the same writ? Why or why not?

Self-check

(1) Sarah can file her own Reply and Request for Hearing within 14 days of receiving the Notice. Her clock runs independently from Carl's — and Carl cannot file for her. (2) Carl must call Sarah today. Her 14-day window is already running; if she misses it, her share may go to a creditor for a debt that has nothing to do with her.

(3) Sarah's $1,180 paycheck is identifiable from the March 10 entry — traceable to her wages, and she is not a judgment debtor. She should claim it in her Reply. This is the core of the joint account analysis: even mixed with Carl's money, her deposits are traceable and protectable. (4) Generally no. A Writ of Garnishment freezes what was in the account on the day of service; later deposits typically aren't subject to the same writ. Carl should confirm with the bank exactly what was frozen and when.

DecideExercise 5 · Which 60(b) grounds apply? (David, Carmen, Robert)▶

For each situation, identify the URCP 60(b) ground(s) and write one or two sentences on the supporting facts. If more than one applies, list all.

(a) David received the complaint, read it, and asked a friend who had "handled stuff like this before" to help. The friend did nothing. The deadline passed; judgment entered. David found out two weeks later.
(b) Carmen was sued at an address she left 14 months ago. She never received the complaint. She learned of the judgment four months after it entered, when her employer told her about the garnishment.
(c) Robert believes he paid the debt in full two years ago. A bank statement showing full payment surfaced only when he was cleaning out old files — after the judgment entered.

Self-check

(a) Ground 1 (excusable neglect) — but a hard sell. David knew about the case and relied on someone else without following up; "I thought someone else was handling it" faces resistance compared to "I never received the papers." No other obvious grounds.

(b) Ground 1 (never received the complaint) AND Ground 4 (if service at the old address was so defective the court never had jurisdiction — void judgment). Critically, four months have passed since entry: the 3-month deadline for Ground 1 has likely expired. Ground 4 has no 3-month cap — if service was truly improper, it may be the only path. Take this to the supervising attorney.

(c) Ground 2 (newly discovered evidence) and possibly Ground 5 (judgment already satisfied). Ground 2 requires that the evidence could not have been found earlier through reasonable diligence — "cleaning out old files" cuts both ways. Flag both grounds for the supervising attorney.

DecideExercise 6 · Gate 2 timing drill▶
The judgment entered on March 3. Maria first learned of it on March 17, when her bank account was frozen. She came to you on April 2.
  1. Is the motion within the three-month hard deadline for Ground 1? Show your work; state the date the window closes.
  2. Does the gap between March 17 and April 2 raise a reasonable-time concern? Why or why not?
  3. Change one fact: the judgment entered December 20. Maria still comes on April 2. What changes for Ground 1, and what remains available?

Self-check

(1) Three months from March 3 = June 3. April 2 is well within the window — Ground 1 is viable on timing. (2) Sixteen days between learning and seeking help is reasonable — the frozen account likely prompted immediate action. The question gets harder the longer the gap between discovery and filing. (3) Three months from December 20 = March 20. April 2 is past it — Ground 1 is time-barred. Grounds 4–6 carry no three-month cap but remain subject to the reasonable-time requirement. Check whether any apply and call the supervising attorney before proceeding.

DecideExercise 7 · Capstone — putting the gates together for Maria▶
Maria has a $3,200 default judgment. She filed her answer when the case began. Eight weeks ago the court held a pretrial conference; Maria missed it — double shift, no childcare — and tried to reach the court by phone before the hearing. The court struck her answer and entered default that day. She learned of the judgment six weeks ago when her account was frozen. She has $200 in savings. She believes she paid $800 toward the original hospital bill before it was sold to Highland Credit, a debt buyer. She is with you today.
  1. Gate 1. Which ground(s) apply? Support each with specific facts.
  2. Gate 2. Timely under the 3-month deadline? Any reasonable-time concern about the six weeks?
  3. Gate 3. State a specific meritorious defense — not just a category — and its supporting facts.
  4. Motion to Stay. Should you file one alongside? What bond could Maria propose, and what argues for reducing it?
  5. Proposed filing. Maria already filed an answer. What, if anything, must accompany the motion?
  6. Settlement checkpoint. What do you do before filing? What are the two questions?

Self-check

(1) Ground 1 — excusable neglect. A specific, concrete reason (work, no childcare) plus an attempt to contact the court before the hearing: stronger than a client who simply forgot. Ground 4 does not apply — she was properly served and answered; the court had jurisdiction. Ground 3 is unlikely on these facts.

(2) Judgment entered 8 weeks ago — inside the ~90-day window, so Ground 1 is still open. Reasonable time is likely fine: she hasn't been sitting on it.

(3) Credit for the $800 payment (much stronger if she can find any proof — bank statements, a receipt, hospital correspondence). Chain of title is a question worth raising since Highland is a debt buyer, though the facts supporting it are thin so far. Yes — she can fairly argue meritorious defenses.

(4) Yes, if she's willing to do the extra paperwork — with garnishment running, even small amounts matter on her paychecks. Her low income ($200 savings) and defenses backed by more than mere allegations both weigh toward a smaller bond.

(5) Likely nothing, or very little — her answer is already on file. If the motion is granted, the court may reinstate the stricken answer or order it re-filed. Confirm the procedure with the supervising attorney; Maria is not in the position of a client who never responded.

(6) Ask Maria whether she has any lump sum to offer; ask the plaintiff what settlement could look like — and whether they will stipulate to the motions. The CJA facilitates communication but agrees to nothing on the client's behalf.

DecideExercise 8 · Carl paid — but the record still shows $6,540▶
Carl and First National Bank settled: he paid $4,200 as full resolution of the $6,540 judgment, confirmed in writing by the bank's attorney — six weeks ago. The court record still shows the judgment unpaid, his credit report still shows $6,540 owed, and his real estate agent says there may be a lien on his house.
  1. What was the bank required to do after receiving Carl's payment?
  2. What can Carl do now, given that the bank hasn't done it?
  3. What documentation should Carl bring you before you file anything?
  4. If a lien was recorded with the County Recorder, what must Carl's motion ask for — and why isn't the court filing alone enough?

Self-check

(1) File a Satisfaction of Judgment with the court — Utah law requires it after payment, and promptly. (2) Carl can file a motion to compel/declare the judgment satisfied. (3) The written settlement confirmation from the bank's attorney, proof of payment (bank statement, wire confirmation, or canceled check), and any correspondence acknowledging the debt is resolved. (4) If an Abstract of Judgment was recorded, the satisfaction must also be filed with the County Recorder in every county where it was recorded — the court filing alone does not release the lien, and Carl's title isn't clear until that step is done. The motion should explicitly ask the court to order it.

PracticeExercise 9 · Patricia's interest rate — the three questions▶
Patricia's $500 loan came from a storefront lender, structured as a straight loan — not a formal deferred deposit (payday) loan. Her loan agreement states 36% annual interest, and the judgment specifies that rate.
  1. Walk the three questions from Utah Code § 15-1-4. Which rate controls Patricia's judgment?
  2. In dollars, what does that rate cost Patricia per year on a $500 judgment?
  3. Why might a defendant with an extremely high contract rate actually be better off with a small claims default judgment?
  4. Is the contract rate set in stone during settlement talks?

Self-check

(1) Q1: formal payday loan? No — it's a straight loan, so move on. Q2: contract case where the judgment specifies a rate? Yes — 36% controls. (Q3 never reached.) (2) $500 × 36% = $180/year in interest alone. (3) On default — especially in small claims — courts often enter just the judgment amount without the contract interest, so the effective rate can drop dramatically. (4) No — interest is negotiable. A creditor can accept a lower rate (even the statutory default) to close the case, especially when the client has real defenses.

Vocabulary

Glossary

Judgment
A court's official decision that your client owes money — an order with the force of law, not a bill.
Supplemental proceeding
The creditor's identification tool: your client must answer questions about income and property, in writing or at a hearing.
Garnishee
The person or company holding your client's money — the employer (wages) or the bank (accounts) — legally required to comply with the writ.
Writ of Continuing Garnishment
Goes to the employer; repeats every pay period until paid, expired (1 year / 120 days), or stopped by court order.
Writ of Garnishment
Goes to the bank; a one-time freeze of whatever is in the account the day the writ is served. No 25% cap.
Disposable earnings
Gross pay minus mandatory deductions only: federal tax, state tax, FICA, Medicare. Voluntary deductions never count.
The $217.50 floor
If weekly disposable earnings are at or below $217.50, nothing can be garnished — regardless of the debt.
Notice of Garnishment and Exemptions
The form served on your client that starts the 14-day Reply clock and lists (partially) the exemptions.
Reply and Request for Hearing
Your client's response to garnishment or execution: claims exemptions or disputes the amount. Must also be delivered to the creditor and garnishee. Does not challenge the judgment itself.
Exemptions
Income and property the law protects from collection — but only if claimed. Not self-executing.
Tracing (FIFO / LIFO)
Methods for identifying which funds in a mixed account are exempt. First-in-first-out spends oldest deposits first; last-in-first-out spends newest first. Use the method that honestly reflects what remains.
Writ of Execution
Orders a sheriff or constable to seize and sell property in your client's own hands. Rare, often used as pressure. Escalate immediately.
Judgment lien / Abstract of Judgment
Recorded with the County Recorder; attaches to real property and blocks sale or refinance until released. Refer lien questions to your supervising attorney.
Motion to Delay (Stay) Enforcement
Pauses collection while a Motion to Set Aside is decided. Buys time only; usually requires a bond.
Motion to Set Aside — URCP 60(b)
Asks the court to undo the judgment. Analyzed through three gates: grounds, timing, meritorious defense.
Meritorious defense
A real defense to the underlying claim — SOL expired, debt paid, wrong amount, wrong person, no chain of title. Hardship, unfairness, and regret don't qualify.
Post-judgment interest
Accrues from the day of entry until paid. Rate depends on the three-question analysis under Utah Code § 15-1-4.
Motion to Renew Judgment
